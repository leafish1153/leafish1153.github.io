Honouring the Missing Children

Truth and Reconciliation Commission Call to Action #76 & the Ongoing Crisis of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls

TRC Call to Action #76 — What & Why

What it asks: Call to Action #76 asks governments, religious institutions, and communities to partner with Indigenous peoples to locate, document, and memorialize burial sites of children who died while attending Canadian residential schools.

Why it matters: For decades, families were denied information about the fate of their children. Properly identifying burial sites creates space for mourning, truth-telling, and community-led commemoration. It is a cornerstone step toward accountability and healing.

Historical Context

From the late 1800s through the late 20th century, thousands of Indigenous children were removed from their homes and placed in residential schools. Designed to assimilate Indigenous children into Euro‑Canadian culture, these institutions frequently subjected children to neglect, abuse, malnutrition, and disease.

Many children died and were buried without proper records. The absence of acknowledgment contributed to intergenerational trauma and a long-standing lack of closure for families and communities.

Missing & Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls (MMIWG2S+)

Definition: MMIWG2S+ refers to the epidemic of violence, disappearances, and murders disproportionately affecting Indigenous women, girls, and Two-Spirit people. This includes loved ones who have been murdered, remain missing, or whose disappeared status has not been adequately investigated.

Root Causes: The violence is not isolated — it is tied to colonial dispossession, systemic racism within institutions (health, justice, policing), economic marginalization, inadequate social services, and gendered violence perpetuated over generations.

Statistics & Scope

  • Indigenous women face violent victimization at rates significantly higher than non-Indigenous women.
  • Cases are underreported and underinvestigated; many families struggle to get information or action from authorities.
  • The National Inquiry into MMIWG (2016–2019) produced a comprehensive report and Calls for Justice addressing systemic failures.

These numbers reflect structural problems rather than random variation — meaning policy and systemic change are needed to protect lives and ensure justice.

Personal & Community Impact

Each case represents a family, a network of relationships, and cultural loss. Communities experience collective grief, fear, and anger; survivors and relatives often report frustration with how investigations are handled and the lack of culturally appropriate supports.

Memorials, art projects, and community-led databases exist to reclaim stories, remember victims, and demand systemic reform.

National Inquiry & Calls for Justice

The National Inquiry into MMIWG concluded with a Final Report that characterized the violence as rooted in colonialism and systemic discrimination, and it issued hundreds of Calls for Justice aimed at governments, institutions, and all Canadians to take specific actions to prevent violence and ensure healing.

Examples of Calls for Justice include improving police responses, increasing Indigenous-led services, and ensuring culturally safe investigations and supports for families.

Where & When — Geographic and Temporal Context

While the crisis is most widely documented in Canada, similar patterns of violence occur in other settler-colonial states such as the United States and Australia. Colonial policies disrupted Indigenous governance and social structures across these regions, contributing to shared vulnerabilities.

Although Indigenous communities have resisted and documented these harms for decades, wide public attention increased in the 2010s through grassroots activism, media coverage, artistic works, and digital advocacy. The National Inquiry (2016–2019) represented a major national reckoning, but many advocates emphasize that implementation of recommendations remains uneven.

Symbols, Memorials, and Cultural Responses

Red Dress Project: Artist Jaime Black’s REDress Project uses empty red dresses to symbolize the lives of missing and murdered Indigenous women and girls. The stark red display communicates absence, loss, and a visual demand for attention.

Other forms of remembrance include vigils, memorials, oral history projects, and dedicated days of recognition (such as May 5 — Red Dress Day).

Art as Advocacy

Artists, filmmakers, and writers have used creative expression to center Indigenous experiences, educate broader audiences, and pressure institutions to act. Creative forms of remembrance make invisible stories visible and foster empathy across communities.

Media Creator Spotlight: Michelle Chubb

Michelle Chubb (@indigenous_baddie on TikTok) combines humor, cultural pride, and storytelling to educate a young audience about Indigenous histories and contemporary issues including MMIWG.

Her strategy—short-form video with relatable delivery—makes complex topics accessible and invites viewers to learn more, share resources, and reflect on systemic injustice.

How Creators Help

  • Documenting stories and amplifying Indigenous voices
  • Bridging formal research and everyday understanding
  • Pushing institutions into public accountability through awareness

Your Role in Reconciliation — Concrete Steps

Learn & Reflect

Read primary sources, survivor testimonies, and research reports. Understand the historical policies that created residential schools and ongoing institutional gaps that contribute to MMIWG.

  • Attend local events and vigils with respect
  • Prioritize Indigenous-led organizations when researching

Share & Advocate

Use your platforms to share accurate information, support Indigenous creators, and encourage peers to learn. Advocacy can include contacting local representatives to demand better resources and culturally safe policing practices.

Support Community-Led Action

Donate to community initiatives, support Indigenous-run services, and volunteer time where appropriate. Beware of exploitative charity and ensure support is Indigenous-led.
