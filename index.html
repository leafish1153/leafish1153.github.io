Truth and Reconciliation Commission: Call to Action #76

Residential Schools Missing Children
Call to Action #76 calls upon the federal government to work with Indigenous communities to identify, document, maintain, and commemorate burial sites of children who died while attending residential schools.

Thousands of Indigenous children were taken from their families and placed into residential schools, where many faced neglect, abuse, and poor living conditions. Many children never returned home, and their deaths were often undocumented or hidden.

This call to action emphasizes truth-telling and remembrance. It seeks to honour the children who were lost and provide families and communities with answers, dignity, and long-overdue recognition.

Research Focus: Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls

What?

Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls refers to the disproportionately high rates of violence, disappearances, and murders experienced by Indigenous women. This violence is rooted in systemic racism, sexism, and colonial policies.

Who?

Indigenous women and girls are the primary victims. Indigenous communities, advocacy groups, and international organizations work to raise awareness and push for justice and systemic change.

Where?

The crisis is most visible in Canada but is also relevant in countries such as the United States and Australia, where Indigenous communities face similar patterns of violence and discrimination.

When?

This has been an ongoing crisis for decades. While families and communities have spoken out for years, national and media attention has increased only in recent times.

Why?

European colonization disrupted Indigenous societies, imposed harmful policies, and created long-lasting systems of discrimination. These structures continue to contribute to violence and lack of accountability today.

Indigenous women are nearly four times more likely than non-Indigenous women to experience violence. The red dress has become a powerful symbol to honour those who are missing or murdered, representing both loss and remembrance.
