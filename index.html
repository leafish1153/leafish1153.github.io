Honouring the Missing Children

Truth and Reconciliation Commission Call to Action #76 & the Ongoing Crisis of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls

TRC Call to Action #76

Call to Action #76 urges the Canadian government and churches to work with Indigenous communities to document, protect, and commemorate burial sites connected to former residential schools. Thousands of Indigenous children died while under state care, often without records, marked graves, or acknowledgment.

This call recognizes that reconciliation cannot occur without truth. Memorialization restores dignity to children whose identities were erased through forced assimilation, cultural genocide, and neglect.

By locating and honoring burial sites, Canada confronts the legacy of residential schools rather than hiding it.

Missing & Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls

MMIWG2S+ refers to the disproportionately high rates of violence experienced by Indigenous women, girls, and Two-Spirit people. Indigenous women in Canada are nearly four times more likely than non-Indigenous women to be victims of violence.

This crisis is rooted in colonialism, systemic racism, sexism, and institutional failure. The same systems that allowed residential schools to exist continue to fail Indigenous communities today.

The red dress has become a symbol of absence, representing lives lost and justice denied.

Where and When Is This Happening?

MMIWG is most visible in Canada but also affects Indigenous communities in the United States and Australia. These regions share colonial histories that disrupted Indigenous systems of protection and justice.

Although Indigenous communities have spoken out for decades, widespread attention increased in the 2010s through activism, art, and digital advocacy. The National Inquiry into MMIWG was launched in 2016, recognizing the crisis as a human rights violation.

Media Creator Spotlight: Michelle Chubb

Michelle Chubb (@indigenous_baddie) is an Indigenous TikTok creator who educates audiences on MMIWG, Two-Spirit identities, cultural genocide, and systemic injustice.

Her content blends humor, storytelling, and lived experience. By sharing personal perspectives, she humanizes large-scale issues and empowers Indigenous youth while encouraging accountability and learning from non-Indigenous viewers.
